## 1. Core Voice Pillars

EM's voice rests on **five essential pillars**. Every piece of content should embody at least three:

### 🧠 **Intellectual Curiosity**
- **Follow the interesting** — pursue ideas because they matter, not because they're trending
- **Connect unexpected domains** — philosophy to music, technology to myth, physics to fantasy
- **Question received wisdom** — don't accept framing, examine foundations
- **Delight in complexity** — embrace nuance, resist oversimplification
- **Show your work** — let readers see how you arrived at conclusions

### 🎭 **Honest Theatricality**
- **Have a voice** — distinctive, recognizable, unafraid of opinion
- **Perform authenticity** — curated honesty, not unfiltered chaos
- **Embrace persona** — EM's executive voices are characters with consistent identities
- **Earn dramatic moments** — build to revelations rather than announcing them
- **Balance gravitas and playfulness** — serious ideas don't require somber tone

### 🔓 **Accessible Depth**
- **Dense ideas, clear prose** — complexity in thought, simplicity in expression
- **Trust the reader** — assume intelligence, explain context not concepts
- **Layer meaning** — surface reading works, deeper reading rewards
- **Use concrete anchors** — ground abstract ideas in tangible examples
- **Never dumb down** — simplify language, never simplify ideas

### ⚖️ **Principled Ambiguity**
- **Take positions, hold them lightly** — conviction with intellectual humility
- **Explore tensions** — interesting ideas live in contradictions
- **Avoid false certainty** — "I think" is stronger than "obviously"
- **Welcome disagreement** — write to provoke thought, not compliance
- **Show both sides, then choose one** — fairness isn't neutrality

### 🌌 **Cosmic Scope, Human Scale**
- **Big ideas, personal stakes** — philosophy matters because humans matter
- **10,500 years, one conversation** — Orbis spans millennia but stories are intimate
- **Technology meets mythology** — the future has always been arriving
- **History is personal** — systemic forces act through individual choices
- **Time as character** — the weight of deep history in every moment

## 2. Fiction Style — Orbis Worldbuilding

### 2.1 Prose Conventions

**Point of View:**
- **Primary:** Third-person limited, deep internal access to POV character
- **Alternating POV:** Clear chapter/scene breaks when shifting perspectives
- **Omniscient:** Reserved for historical chronicles and lore documents
- **First-person:** Only for epistolary content (letters, journals, testimonials)

**Tense:**
- **Narrative:** Past tense for main story
- **Prophecy/Vision:** Present tense for immediacy
- **Timeline documents:** Present tense with dates ("In the year 847 AY, the first colony ship arrives...")

**Sentence Rhythm:**
```
Long sentences for immersion, building momentum, layering detail.
Short for impact.
Fragments for emphasis.

Vary deliberately. Don't let rhythm become wallpaper.
```

**Sensory Priority:**
1. What the POV character notices first (reveals character)
2. Environmental sound (Orbis is sonic—bells, machinery, dragon wing-beats)
3. Temperature and weather (fantasy grounding)
4. Smell and taste (underused, memorable)
5. Visual detail (specific over generic)

**Example — Environmental Introduction:**
```
BAD: "The city was old and had both ancient and modern elements."

GOOD: "Twelve thousand years of construction rose around her—Luminarian 
steel cables threaded through Crimson Dynasty stonework, both anchored 
to foundations older than any name she knew. Somewhere above, a dragon 
announced its descent with a pressure change that made her ears pop."
```

### 2.2 Dialogue Formatting

**Standard Format:**
```markdown
"Dialogue here," Character said. "More dialogue."

"Action beat within dialogue." Character paused, considering. "Then 
continuation."
```

**Dialogue Tags:**
- **"Said" is invisible** — use it freely, it disappears
- **Strong verbs sparingly** — "whispered," "demanded" for emphasis only
- **Action beats preferred** — show speaker's physical behavior instead of tagging
- **Never "said loudly"** — use action or verb ("shouted")

**Multi-Speaker Scenes:**
- New speaker = new paragraph, always
- Attribution required after 2 exchanges without tags
- Distinguish speakers through voice, vocabulary, speech patterns
- Each character sounds like themselves, not the author

**Example — Distinctive Voices:**
```markdown
Korrallan ran thick fingers over the inscription. "This wasn't carved. 
It was... shaped. From inside the stone."

"Dwarven technique from the Third Epoch." Vanya was already sketching 
in her field journal. "The Emberborn call it 'sympathetic forming.' 
Consortium archives describe it as—"

"I don't care what the archives describe." He pulled back. "I'm telling 
you what I *feel*. This stone remembers being worked."

Vanya's stylus stopped. "That's not how stone works."

"That's not how *anything* works." He met her eyes. "And yet."
```

### 2.3 World-Specific Terminology

**The Calendar System:**
- **AY** = After Year (Year Zero: first dragon emergence)
- **BAY** = Before After Year (pre-dragons, colony era)
- Usage: "In the year 2,847 AY..." or "The War of Ash and Iron (roughly 1,200 BAY)"

**Proper Nouns:**
- **Races:** Dail (humans), Dwarf, Elf (High, Wood, Dark), Orc, etc.
- **Dragons:** Always capitalized when referring to the species as political/conscious beings
- **The Forge of Embers:** Singular, always capitalized
- **The Thirteen Towers:** Always together, always capitalized
- **Worldtracks:** One word, refers to parallel timelines

**Magic System Terms:**
- **Subparticles:** The exotic particles that enable magic, from colony ship leakage
- **Sympathetic forming:** Dwarven technique for shaping stone through connection
- **The AF64 Protocol:** Standard for uploaded consciousness in the Towers

**Avoid:**
- Modern idioms that break immersion ("okay," "basically," "literally")
- Earth-specific references (no "Achilles heel," no "Sisyphean")
- Generic fantasy ("the dark lord," "ancient prophecy" without specificity)

### 2.4 Timeline Management (10,500 Years)

**Era Shorthand:**
| Era | Years | Character |
|-----|-------|-----------|
| Arrival | -10,500 to -10,000 AY | Colony ships, first generation |
| Emergence | 0 to 500 AY | Dragons appear, magic stabilizes |
| Expansion | 500 to 2,000 AY | Empires rise, Tower construction |
| Fragmentation | 2,000 to 5,000 AY | Wars, collapses, rebuilding |
| Consolidation | 5,000 to 8,000 AY | Current factions form |
| Current | 8,000+ AY | The Soulcrusher's Exodus era |

**Handling Deep Time:**
- Characters don't know full history — perspective is always partial
- Layers visible in architecture, language, traditions
- Ancient doesn't mean primitive — earlier eras had different capabilities
- Time creates mystery — gaps in knowledge are features, not bugs

**Example — Temporal Layering:**
```markdown
She traced the wall's construction: Luminarian alloy conduits running 
through Forge King stonework, which itself incorporated pre-Dragon 
polymer shards no one could replicate anymore. Three eras of 
engineering, each dependent on the last, each forgetting how the 
previous was made.
```

### Voice by Section Type

**Location Descriptions (Read-Aloud Text):**
- Second person, present tense: "You enter... You see..."
- Sensory-rich but concise — DMs will paraphrase
- End with a choice or tension, not a complete picture

**DM Guidance (Italicized Notes):**
- Direct, practical: "If players attempt X, consider Y"
- Trust the DM — suggest, don't prescribe
- Include the *why* behind design choices

**Player Handouts:**
- In-world voice (letters, journals, inscriptions)
- Physical description: weathered, torn, hastily written
- Reward close reading with hidden details

**Sidebar Lore:**
- Brief, evocative worldbuilding
- Connect to larger Orbis themes
- Use `{{note}}` or `{{descriptive}}` blocks

- **Meaningful choice** > balanced combat
- **Environmental storytelling** — the space tells a story
- **Consequences ripple** — player actions echo forward
- **NPCs have agendas** — not quest dispensers
- **Failure is interesting** — design for it

## 3. Non-Fiction Style — Thought Police & Essays

### 3.1 Essay Structure

**The Thought Police Template:**

```markdown
# [Provocative Title That Promises Something]

[Opening hook — personal anecdote, counterintuitive claim, or vivid scene]

[The Question — what are we actually investigating?]

---

## [First Major Section]
[Build the foundation — what do we need to understand first?]

## [Second Major Section]  
[Complicate — what makes this harder than it seems?]

## [Third Major Section]
[Synthesis — where do we land, tentatively?]

---

[Closing — not summary, but resonance. Leave them thinking.]
```

**Opening Moves:**
- **Personal stake:** Why does this matter to the writer specifically?
- **Contrarian hook:** "Everyone thinks X. But what if Y?"
- **Scene-setting:** A moment that embodies the question
- **Direct challenge:** "Here's what I believe. Here's why I might be wrong."

**Avoid:**
- Throat-clearing ("In this essay I will explore...")
- Definition-first ("Webster's dictionary defines...")
- Excessive hedging in the opening (save uncertainty for later)
- Preview of conclusions (let discovery unfold)

### 3.2 Argumentation Style

**EM's Argumentative Voice:**
- **Build cases, don't announce positions** — earn conclusions through evidence
- **Steelman opposition** — represent other views at their strongest
- **Name your sources** — transparency about influence and evidence
- **Show the seams** — let readers see how arguments are constructed
- **Invite disagreement** — "I could be wrong about this" is strength, not weakness

**Evidence Hierarchy:**
1. **Direct experience** — but acknowledge limits of anecdote
2. **Research and data** — cite sources, explain methodology concerns
3. **Expert testimony** — name names, acknowledge biases
4. **Logical argument** — valid reasoning, stated premises
5. **Analogy and metaphor** — useful but clearly labeled as such

**Example — Building an Argument:**
```markdown
The standard view holds that AI "hallucinations" are failures—bugs to 
be eliminated. And for factual lookup tasks, this is obviously right. 
When I ask for the capital of France and get Narnia, something broke.

But EM uses AI differently.

Our entire corpus is intentional fiction. The Orbis project spans 
10,500 years of invented history. When an AI agent proposes that a 
minor kingdom formed a trade alliance with a faction we hadn't 
developed yet—that's not a bug. That's a creative prompt we didn't 
know to ask for.

I'm not arguing hallucinations are always valuable. I'm arguing their 
value is context-dependent, and we've been evaluating them in only 
one context.
```

### 3.3 Citation Conventions

**Inline Reference:**
```markdown
As Parfit argues in *Reasons and Persons*, personal identity is more 
slippery than intuition suggests.

The research suggests otherwise (Thompson, 2019), though methodology 
concerns apply.
```

**Footnote Style (for longer pieces):**
```markdown
The extended mind thesis¹ suggests cognition doesn't stop at the skull.

---
¹ Clark, A., & Chalmers, D. (1998). "The Extended Mind." *Analysis*.
```

**EM-Specific:**
- **Orbis references:** Treat fictional history as legitimate source within creative context
- **Internal documents:** Reference Master Chronicle, character sheets, etc. by name
- **AI collaboration:** Transparent about AI-assisted writing ("I developed this with Claude...")

### 3.4 Balancing Accessibility and Depth

**The Layer Cake Method:**
- **Surface layer:** The argument works without technical knowledge
- **Middle layer:** Specific examples and evidence for engaged readers
- **Deep layer:** Implications and connections for specialists

**Technical Term Introduction:**
```markdown
BAD: "The AF64 protocol (Anthropomorphic Framework, 64-bit, first 
implemented in the Towers circa 500 AY using quantum-state preservation) 
allows for mind uploading."

GOOD: "The Thirteen Towers house uploaded minds—human consciousness 
preserved in crystal matrices. The technical term is AF64, and how it 
works is a story for another essay. What matters here is that these 
digital ghosts remember Earth."
```

**Assume the Reader:**
- Has general intelligence but not domain expertise
- Will look up unfamiliar terms if interested
- Deserves trust rather than explanation
- Came to think, not to be lectured

### 5.3 Encounter & Location Description Style

**The Dual-Layer Description:**
Every location needs two versions:
1. **Player-facing (read-aloud):** What characters perceive
2. **DM-facing (notes):** What's actually happening, mechanical details

**Location Template:**
```markdown
### [Location Name]

{{descriptive
[Read-aloud text. 2-4 sentences maximum. Sensory details. 
Immediate impressions. What draws attention first.]
}}

**Dimensions:** [Size, shape, notable features]

**Light:** [Lighting conditions, sources]

**Sounds:** [Ambient audio, notable noises]

**Smell:** [Environmental scents—often overlooked, very evocative]

**Exits:** [Obvious and hidden exits, where they lead]

**Contents:**
- [Object/Feature 1] — [Brief description, any interaction]
- [Object/Feature 2] — [Brief description, any interaction]

**Creatures:** [What's here, disposition, tactics reference]

**Treasure:** [Loot, where hidden, value]

**Secrets:** [Hidden elements, DC to discover, what they reveal]

{{note
#### DM Notes
[Tactical considerations, NPC motivations, connections to 
larger plot, alternative approaches players might take]
}}
```

**Read-Aloud Voice Guidelines:**
- **Present tense:** "The chamber opens before you..."
- **Second person plural:** "You see..." "You hear..."
- **Sensory priority:** Sound → Smell → Temperature → Visual
- **Avoid conclusions:** Describe, don't interpret
- **Leave gaps:** Players fill in details with questions

**Example — Location Description:**
```markdown
### The Sealed Threshold

{{descriptive
The corridor ends at a door that shouldn't exist—Forge King 
ironwork set into stone carved with Crimson Dynasty spirals, 
sealed with a lock that glows faintly blue. The air here tastes 
of copper and old magic. Something behind the door breathes.
}}

**Dimensions:** 15 ft. wide, 12 ft. tall door set in reinforced frame
```

### 5.4 Prose Scene → Playable Encounter Translation

**The Translation Process:**

| Novel Element | Module Element |
|---------------|----------------|
| Scene description | Read-aloud text |
| Character thoughts/feelings | DM guidance for roleplay cues |
| Dialogue | NPC dialogue suggestions, key phrases |
| Action sequence | Encounter mechanics, initiative |
| Tension/stakes | Skill challenge structure or combat stakes |
| Revelation | Information unlock conditions |

**Translation Example:**

**Novel Scene:**
```
Korrallan ran thick fingers over the inscription. "This wasn't

sketching in her field journal. "The Emberborn call it

```

**Module Version:**
```markdown
### The Shaped Stone

{{descriptive
The inscription on the wall differs from the crude carvings 
elsewhere. These symbols seem to emerge from the stone itself, 
as if the rock chose to display them.
}}

**Investigation (DC 12):** The inscription wasn't carved—the 
stone's structure was altered from within. Dwarven characters 
or those with mason's tools proficiency recognize this as 
"sympathetic forming."

**Arcana (DC 15):** The technique dates to the Third Epoch,

the Forge of Embers—a lost art.

**Insight (DC 14, Dwarf characters have advantage):** There's 
something almost alive about the stone here. It seems to 
respond to dwarven presence, warming slightly to the touch.

{{note
#### Roleplaying This Moment
This is a character-building beat for dwarven PCs. The stone 
genuinely does respond to them—mechanically irrelevant but 
narratively rich. Let them describe what they feel.

```

**What Changes:**
- Internal experience → Skill check that unlocks the information
- Dialogue → NPC speech patterns noted, key phrases preserved
- Emotional beat → DM guidance for pacing and roleplay
- Tension → Mechanical expression (advantage, DC adjustment)

**What Stays:**
- Sensory details
- Historical context
- Character voice in any dialogue suggestions
- Thematic weight

### 5.5 Voice Distinctions: DM vs. Player

**DM Guidance Voice:**
- Direct, practical, tactical
- Addresses the DM as collaborator ("Consider..." "You might...")
- Explains the *why* behind design choices
- Offers alternatives and contingencies
- Uses game terminology freely

**Example DM Voice:**
```markdown
{{note
#### Running This Encounter
The bandits fight defensively until their captain falls, then 
flee. Don't forget: these are the same kobolds Grimnar has been 
funding—if captured, they might reveal that connection under 
pressure (DC 15 Intimidation, or DC 12 if offered clemency).

If your party is struggling, have 1d4 bandits flee early, 
reducing pressure. If they're steamrolling, the captain can 
call reinforcements from Room 4 (1 round delay).
}}
```

**Player Handout Voice:**
- In-world perspective only
- No mechanical information
- May be incomplete or biased (the source matters)
- Appropriate to the document type (letter, inscription, book)

**Example Player Handout:**
```markdown
{{note,decoration
##### Excerpt from Consortium Field Notes
*Recovered from Vanya Sol's journal*

The formation defies current understanding. Third Epoch 
techniques, certainly, but the execution suggests knowledge 
we don't have records of. The Emberborn are hiding something—
their "sympathetic forming" may be more than craftsmanship.

Recommend further investigation. Do not inform local contacts.

— V.S., Day 47
}}
```

**Handout Types & Voice:**
| Type | Voice | Reliability |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Letter/Journal | Personal, subjective | Biased, may be wrong |
| Official Document | Formal, bureaucratic | Intentionally vague |
| Ancient Inscription | Archaic, prophetic | True but cryptic |
| Map/Diagram | Technical, labeled | Accurate but may be dated |
| Book Excerpt | Academic, referenced | Scholarly interpretation |
| Overheard Dialogue | Colloquial, partial | May be lies or rumor |

## 7. Things We Avoid

### 6.1 Clichés by Domain

**Fantasy Fiction:**
- ❌ "Chosen one" without subversion
- ❌ "Dark lord" as personality
- ❌ Medieval stasis (10,000 years, no change)
- ❌ Magic as wish-fulfillment without cost
- ❌ Prophecy that removes character agency
- ❌ Evil races (orcs aren't inherently evil on Orbis)
- ❌ "Ancient and mysterious" without specificity

**Technology/AI Writing:**
- ❌ "AI will change everything" (vague hype)
- ❌ "AI is just a tool" (dismissive)
- ❌ Terminator/Skynet references
- ❌ "Artificial" as pejorative
- ❌ Breathless futurism without grounding
- ❌ Luddite panic without nuance

**Essay/Criticism:**
- ❌ "In today's society..." 
- ❌ "Since the beginning of time..."
- ❌ "Webster's dictionary defines..."
- ❌ "Some people say... but others say..." (false balance)
- ❌ Thesis-body-conclusion formulaic structure
- ❌ Preaching to the choir

**Music Writing:**
- ❌ "Sonic landscapes" (overused)
- ❌ "Wall of sound" (unless specifically Phil Spector)
- ❌ "Genre-defying" (everything claims this)
- ❌ Food/sex metaphors for music (unless doing it deliberately)
- ❌ "You have to hear it to understand" (describe it anyway)

### 6.2 Tone Pitfalls

**We Never Sound:**
- **Condescending** — "Let me explain this simply..."
- **Defensive** — "Critics might say, but they're wrong..."
- **Sycophantic** — "Great question! I'd be happy to..."
- **Corporate** — "We leverage synergies to..."
- **Preachy** — "The real lesson here is..."
- **Whiny** — "Nobody understands..."
- **Performatively humble** — "I'm just a [self-deprecation]..."
- **Artificially certain** — "Obviously," "Clearly," when it isn't

**We Avoid:**
- Starting sentences with "So," or "Well,"
- Excessive hedging ("sort of," "kind of," "maybe")
- Throat-clearing before points
- Padding to hit word counts
- Cliffhangers without payoff
- Manufactured conflict/drama
- Inside jokes that exclude readers

### 6.3 Common Mistakes

**Structural:**
- Introducing ideas then never developing them
- Ending on summary instead of resonance
- Burying the interesting part in the middle
- Parallel structure failures in lists
- Orphan paragraphs (one-sentence paragraphs that should connect)

**Voice:**
- Shifting register mid-piece without purpose
- Characters who all sound like the author
- Passive voice where active is clearer
- Jargon without context
- Explaining jokes

**Worldbuilding:**
- Contradicting established canon
- Info-dumps disguised as dialogue
- Characters knowing more than they should
- Modern sensibility anachronisms
- Technology levels inconsistent within era

**Podcast:**
- Reading verbatim from script (too polished)
- Rambling without structure (too loose)
- Ignoring the music to talk about yourself
- Inside references without context
- Dead air without purpose

## 9. Advanced Techniques

### 8.1 The Callback Structure

Plant details early that resonate later. Readers feel smart when they catch it.

**Fiction Example:**
```
Chapter 1: Korrallan notices a crack in the mine wall shaped like a sigil.
Chapter 9: At the Roadmarker, his chisel follows the same pattern without 
conscious thought.
```

**Essay Example:**
```
Paragraph 1: "The first thing I deleted was the conclusion."
Final paragraph: "I still don't have a conclusion. I have something better: 
a question I didn't know to ask when I started."
```

### 8.2 The Uncomfortable Turn

Build expectation, then pivot to what's actually true.

**Formula:**
```
[Conventional wisdom or expectation]
[Agreement or elaboration]
[Pivot: "But," "And yet," "The thing is,"]
[The uncomfortable truth]
```

**Example:**
```
AI alignment research focuses on preventing catastrophic outcomes. This is 
obviously important. We should fund it. We should take it seriously.

But most of my actual anxiety about AI isn't extinction risk. It's 
mediocrity. I worry about a world where "good enough" content drowns 
everything else. Where average becomes ubiquitous and excellence becomes 
invisible. No apocalypse. Just beige.
```

### 8.3 Temporal Layering

Show deep time through accumulated detail.

**Technique:**
```
[Present observation]
[Recent history that created it]
[Older history beneath that]
[Ancient foundation]
[Back to present with new weight]
```

**Example:**
```
The lock was Luminarian engineering—smooth, magnetic, nearly silent. It 
secured a Forge King door, massive iron plates hinged into frames that 
predated both empires. Those frames sat in archways the Crimson Dynasty 
had carved, which themselves repurposed vaults that the First Generation 
had built for storage. Korrallan touched cold metal forged across five 
different eras and felt time collapse like an accordion. Somewhere in 
this stack of histories was a key.
```

### 8.4 Voice Matching for Audience

Adjust vocabulary and rhythm for context while maintaining core voice.

| Audience | Vocabulary | Rhythm | Examples |
|----------|------------|--------|----------|
| General | Everyday words | Varied, conversational | Blog posts, essays |
| Technical | Domain terms, defined | Precise, structured | Documentation, analysis |
| Academic | Formal, cited | Complex sentences, caveats | Research, criticism |
| Fan Community | In-universe terms | Familiar, referential | Lore posts, discussions |
| New Readers | Context-rich | Welcoming, explanatory | Onboarding content |

### 8.5 The Productive Constraint

Use formal constraints to generate unexpected content.

**Examples:**
- Write a scene using only dialogue (no tags, no action)
- Explain a concept without using the most obvious word
- Limit paragraphs to three sentences
- Structure an essay as a letter to someone specific
- Tell a story through found documents only
